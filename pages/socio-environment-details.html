Energy consumption and carbon footprints across different geographical regions

This streamgraph illustrates the distribution of energy consumption and carbon footprints across different geographical regions, categorized by the model's parameter size.

Data source: Kaggle Kaggle

The streamgraphs depict a pronounced non-linear escalation in environmental costs as AI model complexity increases, with a marked surge in resource demand for models exceeding 1,000 billion parameters. For mid-sized models, energy consumption is predominantly concentrated in the US East (Northern Virginia) region. In contrast, at the ultra-large scale, the Memphis, Tennessee region accounts for the overwhelming majority of both total energy use and associated carbon emissions. The divergence between the two visualizations further highlights the critical role of regional electricity grid efficiency. Locations characterized by low carbon intensity, notably France and Finland, exhibit a negligible environmental footprint even as computational demands rise. It is important to note, however, that the data for France and Finland consist of only a single observation each; consequently, their contribution is effectively infinitesimal when compared with regions represented by substantially larger numbers of records. The same consideration applies to China, which is represented by only two observations. Overall, this comparison underscores that the sustainability of artificial intelligence systems is shaped not only by algorithmic and hardware efficiency, but also—crucially—by the geographical placement of data centers and the carbon intensity of the local power grids from which they draw energy.
